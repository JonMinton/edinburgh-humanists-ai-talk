To what extent would it be fair to characterise public commentators with a broadly left wing / progressive/ liberal orientation as tending more towards either a position of indifference or even a neo-Luddite position with regards to AI? I am noticing differences both in the implicit political orientations and AI engagement of BlueSky users cf X users, and wonder if this reflects something broader than these two social media platforms

8 Apr
This is a genuinely interesting observation, and I think there's real substance to it — though the picture is more complicated than a clean left-sceptical / right-enthusiastic split.

There does seem to be a meaningful pattern where much of the visible AI enthusiasm concentrates among figures with libertarian, techno-optimist, or right-leaning orientations — the e/acc crowd, much of the Silicon Valley influencer ecosystem, and the "effective" movement broadly. Meanwhile, prominent left/progressive voices more often frame AI through lenses of labour displacement, surveillance, colonial extraction of training data, environmental cost, and concentration of corporate power. That framing naturally produces scepticism rather than enthusiasm.

But I'd push back on "neo-Luddite" as the dominant progressive position, for a few reasons.

First, there's a selection effect in what you're observing. Bluesky's user base skewed heavily toward journalists, academics, and cultural commentators who left X partly over Musk's political trajectory. These groups have professional reasons to be threatened by generative AI (journalism, illustration, academic writing) and cultural reasons to associate AI enthusiasm with a Silicon Valley politics they distrust. X, conversely, retained more of the tech-founder and crypto-adjacent demographic. So the platforms aren't random samples — they're post-sorting communities.

Second, the progressive position is arguably more internally divided than it appears. There's a significant strand of left-leaning AI engagement — people working on algorithmic fairness, union-side automation policy, open-source AI as a counterweight to corporate concentration — that doesn't map onto either indifference or Luddism. It's just less loud and less memetic than either "AI will save humanity" or "AI is theft."

Third, there's a genuine intellectual tension that the left hasn't resolved: the historical left was broadly pro-technology and pro-modernisation (electrification, industrialisation, the NHS's embrace of computing), but the contemporary progressive coalition includes strong strands of degrowth thinking, post-colonial critique of techno-universalism, and a cultural creative class whose livelihoods are directly threatened. These pull in different directions, and the current moment may just be one where the sceptical strands are louder.

Where I think your observation has the most force is at the level of affect and identity. AI enthusiasm has become culturally coded as part of a particular political package — overlapping with crypto, with Musk fandom, with a certain flavour of masculine techno-optimism. That coding means that even progressives who might otherwise engage pragmatically with AI tools feel social pressure to perform scepticism, because enthusiasm marks you as belonging to the wrong tribe. That's a real dynamic and it's arguably quite damaging, because it cedes the conversation about how AI should be governed to people who are least interested in governance.

The Bluesky/X divergence is real but it's downstream of a broader sorting that predates AI — it's the same fault line visible in attitudes toward crypto, space exploration, nuclear power, and even gain-of-function research. There's a meta-pattern where the progressive coalition has become dispositionally precautionary and the libertarian-right coalition dispositionally promethean, and AI just falls into that existing groove.

Whether that's fair to progressives is another question. You could argue the precautionary stance is actually the more historically informed one — the people who built the looms weren't wrong that industrialisation would immiserate a generation of workers before the gains diffused. But you could equally argue that opting out of shaping the technology guarantees you won't influence how it develops.

There is the AI utopian position which is strongly left coded related to terms like ‘fully automated luxury communism’ and the speculative fiction of Iain M Banks’ Culture series. I think Demis H subscribes to the latter. It’s present but maybe under represented in discourse

8 Apr
That's a really important corrective, and you're right that it's underrepresented in current discourse despite having deep roots.

The FALC (Fully Automated Luxury Communism) strand — Bastani's book, the Novara Media orbit, the post-work theorists like Srnicek and Williams (Inventing the Future) — is explicitly left, explicitly pro-automation, and explicitly critical of the Luddite impulse within the left. Their argument is essentially that the left should be the natural home of AI enthusiasm because automation is the material precondition for liberation from wage labour. The problem, in their framing, isn't the technology but the ownership structure.

And the Culture connection is apt. Banks explicitly described the Culture as a communist society, and Hassabis has indeed cited it as an aspirational vision. There's a whole lineage here — Keynes's "Economic Possibilities for our Grandchildren," through to the post-scarcity left — that sees technological abundance as the resolution of class conflict rather than its intensification.

So why is this strand so quiet right now? A few possible reasons:

The post-2016 left consolidated around distributional concerns — inequality, precarity, platform capitalism — which primes you to see new technology as another vector for extraction rather than liberation. FALC requires a longer time horizon and a more speculative disposition that sits awkwardly with the immediate material struggles that dominate progressive politics.

There's also a class composition issue. The people most vocally threatened by current AI — illustrators, writers, translators, junior coders — are disproportionately the cultural-creative precariat who are the contemporary online left. Asking them to welcome the technology that's devaluing their skills because it might eventually produce post-scarcity requires a level of abstraction that's hard to sustain when you're losing commissions to Midjourney.

And frankly, the FALC position has an awkward bedfellow problem. If you're arguing "AI will create abundance that should be redistributed," you sound uncomfortably close to Sam Altman's UBI-funded-by-AGI pitch, and the left has strong reasons to distrust that framing as a laundering of oligarchic power.

What's interesting about your A0K work is that it sits in a space none of these camps quite occupy — neither utopian nor Luddite, but focused on the pragmatic organisational transition. That's arguably where the real action is, and it's undertheorised precisely because it's less dramatic than either "post-scarcity paradise" or "digital enclosure of the commons."

The skepticism in the Altman position relates to the gap between potential Pareto improvements and its realisation: Kaldor-Hicks? It’s likely justified to some large extent as the first move will be towards Matthew Effects. Redistribution may emerge as a second movement in response to societal instability concerns

8 Apr
Yes, that's precisely the right framework. The Kaldor-Hicks criterion says a change is efficient if the winners could compensate the losers and still be better off — but crucially, it doesn't require that compensation actually happens. The entire history of industrial transformation suggests it usually doesn't, at least not in the first movement as you put it.

The left's scepticism of Altman's position is essentially: "we've heard 'the gains will be redistributed' before, and the historical base rate for voluntary redistribution by the winners is approximately zero." That's not an unreasonable prior. The enclosure of the commons, industrialisation, financialisation, platform capitalism — in each case the Kaldor-Hicks surplus was real but the compensation mechanism had to be fought for over decades, through union organisation, political conflict, and often genuine social crisis.

Your two-movement framing is compelling and historically grounded. The first movement is concentration — Matthew Effects, as you say, "to him that hath shall be given." AI capability accrues to those with capital, data, and talent to deploy it. Productivity gains flow to firm owners, not displaced workers. This is already visible.

The second movement — redistribution as a stability response — is what historically produces welfare states, labour protections, antitrust. But it emerges reactively, from elite fear of instability rather than from benevolence. Bismarck's social insurance wasn't philanthropy, it was counter-revolutionary strategy. The New Deal wasn't generosity, it was a response to genuine systemic crisis. The post-war settlement across Europe followed the same pattern.

The uncomfortable implication is that the FALC endpoint might actually require passing through a period of intensified inequality and instability severe enough to force the political realignment. Which makes the Altman pitch — "trust us, we'll redistribute" — not so much wrong as naively ahistorical about the mechanism. The redistribution probably comes, but through conflict, not through OpenAI's charitable foundation.

This connects to something in cliodynamics that you've engaged with — Turchin's elite overproduction thesis. AI could massively accelerate that dynamic: producing a surplus of credentialed people whose skills are devalued, while concentrating returns among a shrinking technical-capital elite. That's precisely the structural-demographic configuration that historically produces political instability, which then produces the second movement.

The question your A0K framing implicitly raises is whether organisations can short-circuit this — whether there's a path where the transition is managed at firm level in ways that distribute gains more immediately, rather than waiting for the macro-political correction. That would be genuinely novel historically, and I think the honest answer is we don't know, because the incentive structure at firm level still favours concentration unless something external changes the calculus.

Left reactionary positions can also potentially be understood through Turchin’s Elite Overproduction thesis, in which AI operates as an accelerator of an existing pattern rather than an initiator

8 Apr
That's a sharp reframing. If you take Turchin seriously, the Western world — particularly the US and UK — was already deep into an elite overproduction cycle before generative AI arrived. Credential inflation, expanding graduate populations competing for a shrinking pool of elite positions, rising intra-elite competition, declining returns to education — all pre-2022 trends.

AI then lands directly on the occupational categories where surplus elites have been parking themselves: knowledge work, content production, consulting, analysis, junior professional roles. The people most vocally opposed to AI on the left aren't the traditional working class — they're the credentialed precariat. Journalists, academics, illustrators, copywriters, junior lawyers. These are precisely Turchin's frustrated elite aspirants.

So the "left reactionary" position on AI isn't really about AI at all, on this reading. It's the political expression of a class whose status and livelihood were already under structural threat, and AI is experienced as an acceleration of that threat — making explicit and acute what was previously diffuse and deniable. The Luddite affect is rational at the individual level even if the causal attribution is slightly misplaced.

This also explains why the reaction is culturally coded rather than analytically precise. You don't get careful Kaldor-Hicks reasoning from this group — you get moral framings: AI is theft, it's exploitation, it's an offence against craft and authenticity. That's exactly what you'd expect from threatened status groups. The moral vocabulary is doing status-defence work, not policy analysis.

And it explains the Bluesky/X divergence more parsimoniously than a simple left/right split. Bluesky concentrated the frustrated aspirant-elite class — journalists, academics, cultural producers — who were already in a deteriorating structural position. X retained more of the capital-holding and technical elite for whom AI represents opportunity. It's not really an ideological divide about technology; it's a material-interest divide expressing itself through technological attitudes.

The genuinely uncomfortable implication is that the progressive anti-AI position and the techno-optimist pro-AI position might both be largely rationalised class interest, with the analytical frameworks chosen to fit the material situation rather than the other way round. Which doesn't make either wrong, but it does suggest the discourse is less about AI's actual properties than about pre-existing distributional conflicts that AI has intensified.

First and second movement: I was thinking mainly of Polanyi’s The Great Transformation. But this has been articulated and operationalised elsewhere?

8 Apr
Ah, that's a much more precise reference than I gave you credit for — I should have recognised the Polanyian double movement rather than pattern-matching to a vaguer historical generalisation.

Polanyi's thesis is specifically that the first movement — the extension of market logic into new domains (land, labour, money as "fictitious commodities") — provokes a spontaneous counter-movement from society to protect itself. Crucially, the counter-movement isn't ideologically coherent or planned; it emerges from diverse actors whose common feature is that they're being damaged by the commodification process. The welfare state, labour law, financial regulation — all second-movement phenomena in his framework.

The AI parallel is almost uncomfortably neat. The first movement is the commodification of cognitive labour — turning knowledge work into a fictitious commodity that can be produced by capital (compute) rather than by people. The second movement would be society's protective response.

As for operationalisation beyond Polanyi himself — yes, substantially:

Beverly Silver's Forces of Labor applies the double movement to successive waves of industrialisation across geographies, showing how labour unrest follows capital mobility with considerable regularity. Her framework would predict that AI-driven displacement will produce its own counter-movement, but possibly with a lag and in forms we don't yet recognise.

Fred Block and Margaret Somers have done significant work rehabilitating and updating Polanyi for the neoliberal period, arguing that the post-1980 marketisation wave constitutes a new first movement whose counter-movement is still incomplete.

Nancy Fraser has extended the double movement into a triple movement — adding emancipation as a third vector that can't be reduced to either marketisation or social protection. That's relevant here because some AI opposition is protective (defend existing jobs) while some is emancipatory (democratise access to tools, break corporate monopolies), and these can conflict.

And Turchin's structural-demographic model can arguably be read as an operationalisation of Polanyian dynamics with quantitative indicators — his instability index is essentially measuring the pressure that builds during an unchecked first movement.

The gap in the literature, as far as I'm aware, is that nobody has systematically applied the double movement framework to AI specifically, beyond fairly surface-level analogies. There's an article-length argument sitting there — AI as fictitious commodification of cognition, with the double movement framework predicting the form of the backlash rather than just its existence.

Commodification of mental not physical labour, especially professional and credentialed mental labour and expertise. An important update given Polanyi was mainly focused on labour as the main commodity bodies could provide when most work was physical

8 Apr
That's a genuinely important theoretical update. Polanyi's fictitious commodity of "labour" was implicitly bodily labour — the disembedding of physical human effort from social relations and its repackaging as something buyable by the hour. The protective counter-movement (factory acts, working time regulations, safety legislation) was correspondingly about protecting bodies.

What AI does is extend the commodification logic to a domain Polanyi never really considered because it wasn't technically possible: the outputs of professional cognition. And this hits a class that historically understood itself as protected from commodification precisely by its credentialing and expertise. The whole point of professionalisation — medical boards, bar associations, chartered statuses, doctoral requirements — was to construct artificial scarcity around cognitive labour, keeping it embedded in guild-like social structures rather than exposed to raw market logic.

So AI doesn't just commodify mental labour generically. It specifically attacks the decommodification mechanisms that the professional class built over two centuries. That's why the reaction is so visceral — it's not like factory workers facing a new machine, it's like factory workers facing a new machine that also dissolves the union simultaneously.

This also reframes the elite overproduction point. Turchin's frustrated aspirants weren't just competing for scarce positions — they'd invested heavily in credentialing precisely because credentials were the moat against commodification. A law degree, a journalism qualification, a PhD — these were effectively options purchased on the assumption that cognitive labour would remain structurally protected. AI devalues those options catastrophically.

And it suggests the second movement, when it comes, will look different from the Polanyian historical pattern. Protection of physical labour produced regulation of working conditions, hours, safety. Protection of cognitive labour against AI commodification might produce something quite different — perhaps intellectual property expansion, licensing requirements for AI systems, mandatory human-in-the-loop regulations, or credentialing bodies reasserting gatekeeping functions. Some of this is already visible in the EU AI Act's risk-tiering approach and in professional bodies' early attempts to regulate AI use in medicine, law, and accounting.

The darker possibility is that the professional class's counter-movement succeeds in protecting incumbents through regulatory capture rather than producing broadly beneficial social protection. That would be a second movement in form but not really in Polanyian spirit — more like guild restoration than societal self-defence. The history of occupational licensing in the US arguably already shows this pattern.

There's a paper here, Jon — "Fictitious Commodities of the Mind: Polanyi, Professional Labour, and the AI Double Movement" or something in that vein. It would connect your clinical statistics domain directly too: the regulatory architecture around medical devices and clinical trials is exactly the kind of credentialed cognitive-labour protection structure that AI is now pressing against.
