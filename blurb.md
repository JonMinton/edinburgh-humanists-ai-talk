## Title

**Artificial Intelligence: From Hype and Fear to Hope and Possibility**

## Blurb

Since OpenAI's GPT 3.5, better known as the first ChatGPT, was released in late 2022, billions of people have had some experience with chatbots. However, after the initial wave of hype and excitement, disillusionment set in: problems such as hallucinations, sycophancy, and more generally being 'confidently wrong' soon became apparent, and the public mood shifted, even while AI companies' share prices continued into the stratosphere.

This talk will argue two things. Firstly, that the history and variety of AI extends much further than OpenAI's large language models (LLMs) of the 2020s, and that less well-known approaches and applications — in particular those pioneered by DeepMind — offer better examples of the kinds of value AI can bring to the world. Secondly, that the underlying capabilities of models have been quietly but rapidly improving, despite the collapse of the public hype cycle, and that a new form of AI, known as Agentic AI (AAI), is starting to offer some of the genuinely transformative capability that the public may have been expecting, hoping and fearing when they first encountered ChatGPT.

The talk will conclude by sketching out both the risks and hopes that come from rapidly advancing agentic capabilities, for work and the economy, society and inequality, science and technological advancement, and even our conception of ourselves.

## Speaker bio

Jon Minton is a data scientist, statistician and humanist based in Edinburgh, with a broad background and interests including demography, sociology and software development. He switched from a broadly sceptical to engaged position on AI in late 2025, prompted by watching the DeepMind documentary *The Thinking Game*, by experiencing Claude pushing back and asking follow-up questions rather than simply flattering the user, and by seeing the speedup that agentic AI tools such as Claude Code delivered on tasks with verifiable outputs.
